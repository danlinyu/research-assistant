# Literature Search

## Search Order

Move from broad discovery to targeted retrieval:

1. Clarify concepts and synonyms from the interview stage.
2. Search broad academic indexes.
3. Search domain-specific databases.
4. Pull accessible full texts where possible.
5. Use citation chaining to expand and refine.

## Typical Public Sources

Pick the sources that match the field. Examples:

- Google Scholar
- Crossref
- PubMed and PubMed Central
- Europe PMC
- arXiv
- SSRN
- DOAJ
- institutional repositories
- publisher pages that expose abstracts

Use domain-specific databases when the field demands them, but prefer sources the user can access publicly when possible.

## Query Construction

Build searches from:

- core phenomenon
- population or sample
- outcome
- method or design
- synonyms and related terms
- exclusions for obvious false positives

Template:

```text
("concept A" OR synonym1 OR synonym2)
AND
("concept B" OR synonym3)
AND
(population OR setting)
AND
(method OR design)
```

## Evidence Capture

For each candidate paper, record:

- full citation
- DOI or stable URL
- abstract availability
- full-text availability
- study setting
- data source
- method
- key findings
- limitations
- relevance to the proposed study

## Citation Integrity

- Verify bibliographic details before citing.
- Do not cite from memory.
- If one source cites another important paper, locate the original paper before relying on the claim.
- Mark clearly when a paper was assessed from abstract only.
