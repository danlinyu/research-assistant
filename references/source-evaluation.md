# Academic Source Evaluation

## Evidence Hierarchy

Rank evidence roughly in this order unless the field requires a different convention:

1. Peer-reviewed articles with transparent methods and accessible underlying evidence.
2. Author manuscripts, working papers, and preprints with strong methodological detail.
3. Systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and high-quality review articles.
4. Conference papers, technical reports, dissertations, and institutional working papers.
5. Editorials, viewpoints, blog summaries, and non-academic commentary.

Do not treat peer review as a substitute for critical reading.

## Fast Appraisal Checklist

For each paper, check:

- Authenticity: Can the paper be verified through DOI, publisher page, repository page, or indexing record?
- Status: Peer-reviewed article, accepted manuscript, preprint, working paper, or abstract only?
- Transparency: Are the data, sample, methods, and limitations described clearly enough to assess?
- Relevance: Does it actually address the research question, population, and setting at issue?
- Recency: Is the publication year still appropriate for the topic?
- Replication and corroboration: Do other credible studies converge or conflict?

## Conflict Handling

1. Align constructs, operational definitions, populations, and time periods before comparing findings.
2. Check whether the disagreement comes from design differences, sampling, measurement, or estimation.
3. Separate causal claims from correlational findings.
4. Prefer the source with clearer identification, stronger data, and better transparency.
5. If the literature remains split, report the split honestly and explain why.

## Abstract-Only Use

Abstract-only papers may still be useful for mapping a field, but treat them as limited evidence. Do not overstate details that are not available from the abstract.

When only the abstract is accessible:
- cite only claims supported by the abstract itself
- avoid detailed methodological claims unless the abstract includes them
- mark the paper as abstract-only in the literature matrix

## Retractions And Red Flags

Downgrade or exclude sources when you find:
- retraction, expression of concern, or obvious citation mismatch
- no verifiable bibliographic record
- methods too vague to evaluate
- unsupported claims beyond the data
- duplicate publication or suspiciously recycled text
- mismatch between abstract claims and full-text findings
